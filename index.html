Suicide Rates & Social Media Impact

An examination of suicides across several decades, countries and generations that also looks at the correlation between social media use and suicide rates in the U.S. in more recent years among younger generations. Researchers continue to study a potential causal link between social media use and mental health diseases, including suicides and suicidal tendences. A growing number of studies link frequent social media use to a loss of sleep, depression, anxiety, low self-esteem and a feeling of loneliness.

Suicide is a serious societal problem and the result of complex socioeconomic and cultural factors. Even if our analysis should show that social media use only has minimal impact, we believe any measure to decrease risk and increase suicide prevention is worth pursuing.

Our project is split in two parts: a thorough analysis of a public Kaggle dataset of suicides between 1985 and 2015, and the overlay of a U.S. social media use dataset by Pew Research with data from 2005 through 2015.

Our Github repository includes additional details on our data cleaning and analysis process, as well as our two multivariate regression models for Machine Learning

Analysis Goals

We want to analyze how suicide rates changed over the decades and across different generations to determine specific patterns and identify high-risk groups for suicide. We plan to use two types of regression analyses to determine if our algorithm can accurately predict suicide rates among the different generations we identify.

We also want to determine whether an uptick in social media use in recent years overlaps with an increase in suicide rates, particularly among younger cohorts who use social media in the greatest numbers. To determine correlation, we plan to visualize U.S. suicide rates and social media use over time.

Analysis Findings

We found that global suicide rates have decreased since their peak in 1995, but particularly high suicide rates continue to be driven by countries of the former Soviet Union. Older generations and particularly older people above the age of 75 are at the highest risk for suicide. While the suicide rate of the oldest age group has dropped steadily since 1995, those older than 75 remain significantly more likely to commit suicide. Males continue to be at siginificantly higher risk of committing suicide than females, with the male-to-female suicide ratio being particularly high among Boomers, Gen X and Millennials.

When it comes to U.S. social media use and its relationship to U.S. suicides, we have not been able to establish a connection based on the limited data we have (more details in our limitations section). In fact, there appears to be an inverse relationship based on our data, with an increase in U.S. social media running counter to the number of suicides in each generation and age group. Furthermore, social media use does not explain the high suicide rates among the Silent Generation (those born between 1928 and 1945) whose social media consume is very low.
